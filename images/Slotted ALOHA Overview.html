Slotted ALOHA is a network protocol used for sharing a communication channel among multiple users efficiently. It improves upon the original ALOHA protocol by reducing the chance of data collisions.

What Slotted ALOHA Does:

  • Divides time into equal slots (hence "slotted").

  • Users can only send data at the beginning of these time slots.

  • If two users transmit in the same slot, a collision occurs and both packets are lost.

  • If only one user transmits in a slot, the data is successfully received.

Key Features:

  • Better efficiency than Pure ALOHA: Maximum throughput is 37% (vs. 18% for Pure ALOHA).

  • Synchronization required: All users must align their transmissions to time slots.

  • Still probabilistic: No guarantee of success, just improved odds.

Used in:

  • Early wireless networks

  • Satellite communication systems

📻 CSMA Rule:

“Listen before you talk.”

  • If yes, you wait.

  • If no one is talking, you grab the mic and speak.

This avoids most collisions.

Types of CSMA (bonus but easy):

  1. 1️⃣ CSMA with Collision Detection (CSMA/CD) – like in wired networks:

    • You speak and listen at the same time.

    • If you hear another voice while talking (collision), you stop immediately, wait, then retry.

  2. 2️⃣ CSMA with Collision Avoidance (CSMA/CA) – like in Wi-Fi:

    • You check, then say “Hey, I’m about to talk!” (send a signal).

    • Others stay quiet until you’re done.

🆚 Compared to ALOHA:

  • ALOHA is like shouting whenever.

  • Slotted ALOHA is like shouting on your turn.

  • CSMA is like checking first, then talking = way fewer collisions.

📧 SMTP (Simple Mail Transfer Protocol)

  • Used to send emails from client to server or server to server.

  • Push protocol – it pushes mail to the receiver’s mail server.

  • Works on port 25 (or 587 for secure submission).

  • Only handles sending, not receiving or reading (POP3/IMAP do that).

  • Used by mail clients (like Outlook, Gmail) to send outgoing mail.

📁 FTP (File Transfer Protocol)

  • Used to transfer files between a client and server.

  • Requires login credentials (username & password).

  • Works on port 21 (control), and 20 (data transfer).

  • Supports uploading and downloading files.

  • Can be used via GUI clients (like FileZilla) or command line.

🌐 HTTP (HyperText Transfer Protocol)

  • Used to access web pages on the internet.

  • Works on port 80 (HTTPS uses port 443).

  • Stateless protocol – doesn’t remember past interactions.

  • Transfers HTML, CSS, images, etc. between browser and server.

  • Foundation of data communication on the World Wide Web.

🔌 Guided Media

  • Transmission medium where signals travel through a physical path (like cables).

  • Used in wired communication.

  • Offers higher bandwidth and less interference compared to unguided media.

🧵 Twisted Pair Cable

  • Two insulated copper wires twisted together.

  • Used in telephone lines and LANs (Ethernet).

  • Types:

    • UTP (Unshielded Twisted Pair) – cheaper, common in LANs.

    • STP (Shielded Twisted Pair) – has shielding for less interference.

  • Max range: Around 100 meters.

📺 Coaxial Cable

  • Has a central copper core surrounded by insulation and shielding.

  • Used in cable TV and broadband internet.

  • More noise-resistant than twisted pair.

  • Higher bandwidth than twisted pair.

💡 Optical Fiber Cable

  • Uses light signals to transmit data.

  • Made of glass or plastic fibers.

  • Very high speed, long distance, and immune to EMI (electromagnetic interference).

  • Types:

    • Single-mode – long-distance, narrow light path.

    • Multi-mode – short-distance, wider path.

📘 What are Rules of Inference?

  • Rules of inference are logical tools used to derive new statements (conclusions) from given statements (premises).

  • Used in mathematical proofs, logical reasoning, and algorithms.

  • Ensure that the conclusion is valid if the premises are true.

✅ Common Rules of Inference:

Rule NameLogical FormMeaning / Example
Modus PonensIf P → Q, and P is true ⇒ Q is true"If it rains, I get wet. It rains ⇒ I get wet"
Modus TollensIf P → Q, and ¬Q ⇒ ¬P"If I study, I pass. I didn’t pass ⇒ I didn’t study"
Hypothetical SyllogismIf P → Q and Q → R ⇒ P → R"If A causes B, and B causes C ⇒ A causes C"
Disjunctive SyllogismP ∨ Q, and ¬P ⇒ Q"Either coffee or tea. No coffee ⇒ must be tea"

ResolutionP ∨ Q, ¬P ∨ R ⇒ Q ∨ RUsed in propositional logic and AI (like SAT solvers)

🧠 In Short:

Rules of inference are like legal moves in logic — if you follow them, your conclusions are guaranteed to be correct if your premises are correct.
